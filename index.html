QUEENSSCRIPTORIUM

Can the NYPD Make Better Use of Proposed 116th Precinct?

By Hiram Alejandro Durán

Most Queens’ Community District 13 board members are overjoyed at the prospect of adding a new NYPD precinct to the 12.6-mile area, stretching the length of Southeast, Queens. The 116th precinct comes four decades after residents first started petitioning city officials to lessen crime in the region.

But residents like Kenneth Shelton, a Community Complaints Review Board investigator, question whether or not a new precinct is what the community needs most. “I think if you just got everyone in the community and just asked, ‘hey we got 70-million dollars, what would you want to spend it on?’ I don’t think people would say a new precinct,” said Shelton.

Mayor Bill de Blasio’s main claim for cementing the 116th stems from slow response-times by the 105th to the southern most part of their patrol area. The 105th, located in Queens Village, is one of the cities largest and most encompassing precincts but has perpetually failed to meet adequate response-time standards.

In 2016, the NYPD sought to mitigate response-time issues by establishing an auxiliary post in Laurelton with 20 new NYPD officers. However, the auxiliary post, located at 242-40 North Conduit Ave, will be demolished to make way for the 116th.

“The future 116th Precinct will help reduce current response times in Southeast, Queens and expand coverage throughout the community,” said de Blasio during a press conference last year.

Yet data collected from NYC Citywide Agency Performance Mapping shows other, smaller, districts in Queens have perennially faced longer response times. Districts’ 8, served by 107th, and 10, served by 106th, average the longest response time at over six and a half minutes.

Since 2012, all but neighboring 111th have experienced fluctuating response times.

In recent years, Queens’ residents have seen both some of their quickest response-times, 4.6-minutes in 2014, and some of their slowest response-time 5.5 minutes. NYPD’s Deputy Commissioner, Public Information office declined to comment on the slowing response-times.

Alex Vitale, Policing and Social Justice coordinating professor at Brooklyn College, believes if the city truly wanted to aid communities of color, it would invest in governmental aid that tackles a communities needs through rehabilitation services, education, economic, and health support.

“The problem is that for generation’s communities of color have only been given one resource to deal with those problems and that’s policing,” said Vitale. “Once we define the communities problems in terms of something the police can do to fix them we are basically expanding these kinds of punitive interactions that even if it brings some kind of order it comes with collateral consequences.”
